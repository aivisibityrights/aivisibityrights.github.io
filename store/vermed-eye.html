Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about VerMed Eye

Which IOL formulas does VerMed Eye support?
VerMed Eye implements four theoretical IOL formulas: SRK/T (Retzlaff 1990) for all axial lengths, Holladay 1 (1988) for normal eyes, Haigis (2000) for short and long eyes with preop ACD measurement, and Hoffer Q (1993) for short eyes (<22 mm). It also includes a toric IOL vector calculator with residual astigmatism and axis misalignment penalty. All IOL output is for reference only — surgeons must use biometry device output + manufacturer-optimized A-constants for surgery.
How accurate are the calculations?
Every calculation is coded directly from the original peer-reviewed paper. The app ships with 103 numerical verification tests that validate each engine against textbook values (normal eye 23.5 mm AL, K 44 D → SRK/T emmetropia ~19.6 D; 20/40 Snellen → 70 ETDRS letters; etc.). No AI, no approximations — just the formulas.
Does it work without internet?
Yes, 100% offline. All formulas, reference ranges, OCT norms, and citations are bundled into the app. Nothing is transmitted to any server. No patient data collection, no tracking, no accounts. Open and use it anywhere — in the OR, in the clinic, in rural areas with no connectivity.
What about glaucoma assessment tools?
The app includes Hodapp-Parrish-Anderson visual field staging (early/moderate/severe), OHTS 5-year conversion risk nomogram (Gordon 2002) for ocular hypertensives, cup-to-disc interpretation with asymmetry + disc hemorrhage flagging, Van Herick angle grading, Goldmann CCT correction (Ehlers, Dresdner, Kohlhaas), target IOP by disease stage (AGIS/EMGT/CNTGS/OHTS), diurnal IOP fluctuation analysis (Asrani 2000), and ocular perfusion pressure (MOPP/SOPP/DOPP with Leske 2007 risk thresholds).
Who is this app for?
Ophthalmologists, optometrists, ophthalmology residents and fellows, medical students on eye rotations, and optometric assistants. The Guide pages explain each calculator in plain language — making the app a learning tool as well as a reference. Target users globally: ~230K ophthalmologists + ~430K optometrists + ~30K residents/fellows.
What is the "4-2-1 rule" for diabetic retinopathy?
The 4-2-1 rule defines severe non-proliferative diabetic retinopathy (NPDR) per the ICO / Wilkinson 2003 classification. It is met when ANY ONE of the following is present: (a) intraretinal hemorrhages of 20 or more in each of 4 quadrants, (b) venous beading in 2 or more quadrants, or (c) prominent intraretinal microvascular abnormalities (IRMA) in 1 or more quadrant. VerMed Eye's DR staging tool asks these questions and stages automatically.
Can I use this app during surgery?
VerMed Eye is a clinical reference tool, not a surgical device. IOL power calculations are for reference only — surgical decisions must be made using the actual output from your optical biometry device (IOLMaster, Lenstar, Argos, etc.) with manufacturer-optimized A-constants. Use the app for pre-operative planning cross-checks, post-operative residual refractive analysis, and resident education.
Is this FDA-cleared?
No. VerMed Eye is exempt from FDA medical device regulation under the 21st Century Cures Act §520(o)(1)(E) — it is a clinical reference tool, not a medical device. It does not diagnose, treat, or monitor a specific patient. It provides generalized educational calculations for trained clinicians to interpret in the context of the full clinical picture.
